1.1:
Describing the purpose of IT in modern business

The purpose of IT in modern business is to leverage the advantages of computer technology to enhance efficiency, scalability, and productivity, whilst reducing the costs entailed by more traditional clerical processes [2, 3]. IT has, over the last few decades, assumed a pivotal role in the modern business environment [1]; irrespective of how small or traditional a company may be, they will generally have to invest in IT infrastructure just to remain competitive and relevant [4]. IT will usually be used in all areas of a modern business, from engaging with customers to managing resources both human and logistical. Mindful of its public profile, a business will generally seek to maintain a presence on the web, both through its own website/web application and social media.

Email and instant messaging platforms have massively increased the easy exchange of data and improved the communicative efficiency of teams and organisations. Databases and cloud storage have dramatically reduced the reliance on traditional, spatially burdensome physical data storage. These are just a few examples of how IT has become virtually inseparable from modern business [1, 2].

1.2:
Describing methods, skills and resources needed to complete digital projects successfully – what do you need to make projects successful? What tools? What skills?

Digital projects, especially those involving multiple inputs - ie, most in the real world - can grow large and very complex very quickly; therefore, beyond the technological know-how required for actually constructing software, it is imperative that a team thinks carefully about how it plans and organises its project [6] and how communication and task management is handled during its completion, so long as any planning is done efficiently and doesn’t over-dominate developers’ time [6]. Stand up meetings help teams to strike this balance effectively; they are characterised by their relative brevity and tend to maintain a simple 3-way focus: “what did each team member do yesterday?”, “what will they do today?”, and “what, if any, obstacles/blockers are they dealing with right now?” [7].

The team needs to understand what the client wants and what problems this requires them to solve; they must have some high-level idea of how best to structure the project and delegate tasks to handle this in a reasonable timeframe [1]. Trello [2] is an approachable and effective tool for monitoring progress interactively, as it can be updated quickly and flexibly; used in conjunction with Kanban (the division of tasks into the categories, “to-do”, “doing”, and “done”) [3], it can massively increase the effectiveness of development teams.

Version control using software like Git [4] is also very important for tracking change in large projects; this allows teams to work on multiple sub-parts of a single project simultaneously in sandboxed isolation from the main implementation (branching [4]), into which changes can subsequently be integrated after successful development and testing. This permits any mistakes to be reversed in a safe manner and allows for snapshots of the project to be made at various phases of development; these can be reverted back to in the future should the need arise [5].

1.4:
Describe the risks that might impact digital projects – what causes digital projects to fail? Why?

There are a number of factors that can put at risk the success of an IT project. A lack of clarity concerning the objectives of the project and inadequate communication between clients, managers, and developers can lead to a mismatch between expectations and the end product. Failure to provide realistic timescales and allocate the correct resources can have the result that a developer team finds that it doesn’t have the capacity to meet deadlines or lacks the technical expertise or development tools required [1, 2].

While it may be tempting to prioritise speed of development, proper testing is vital to delivering working, bug-free software; if this is not done, a finished product may be delivered which is superficially functional but fails in deployment, damaging the reputation of the software provider responsible and potentially resulting in a breach of contract [2]. Another potential point of failure which can sabotage the success of a project from the very beginning is choosing technology which does not suit the task required [3].

1.5:
Describe how you would go about selecting and using IT systems and software – how do you select the right kind of technology for the job?

The best tool for use in a given situation arises as a function of the problem to be solved itself [3] and the available budget and technical resources. It is important to consider whether the technology being proposed has proven effective in tackling similar problems previously, whether there will be any financial cost for its use, and whether it is compatible with the platforms being used in production and eventual deployment [1].

It is important to determine whether the technology will be performant given the client’s expected requirements; if it is likely the project will require alteration in future, it may be worth considering how the choice of technology allows for scaling and extensibility. Another consideration is how widely used and supported the technology is: is it likely, in the event of some unforeseen issue emerging in the more distant future, that the client will be able to find the expertise required to solve this issue? These are some of the questions that would need considering before choosing which technology to go with [1].

Were I required to make this decision, I would check what has been used successfully in the past for similar problems, and I would attempt to balance appropriateness (eg, Python for machine learning [2]) and requirements; whichever solution met the most criteria - and entailed the least disadvantage - would then be selected.

1.7
Describe legal guidelines and constraints that impact digital projects – How does GDPR impact digital projects?

It is important for those who are creating software for anything besides personal use to bear in mind the legal realities that pertain to development.

When the production of software is done according to a contract, there are likely to be SLAs (Service Level Agreements) specified concerning timescales, requirements, and other client specifications; to be in breach of these can involve the business’ losing money, clients, and vital prestige; serious enough breaches can be a legal matter, so it is important for projects to be managed with a view to preventing this [1]. Another concern is the use of licensed software in building the project; it can be very easy, particularly given the increasing abundance of open-source resources available, to simply assume that one can use them as one wishes; the reality is otherwise; it is important to consider the usage rights specific to all digital technology used, giving necessary credit and displaying licencing information where mandated [1].

Europe’s new General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR) [4] involves developers in several new considerations; though, in many ways, what it enforces has long been best practice. These regulations apply not just within Europe, but to any instance in which the personal data of EU citizens is handled [2]. When developing user-facing software, it is important only to collect that information for which there is a genuine business need, to make clear to the user the nature of this need, and to retain this data only for so long as the need persists. This naturally has strong ramifications where data is gathered through cookies [5]; where websites gather customer data in this and similar ways, they must make this known and gain the consent of the customer before proceeding to do so [2, 6].

Where personal data is being communicated through digital channels, it is important that every measure available be taken to ensure that this is done in the most secure way technologically possible, eg, using the HTTPS protocol instead of HTTP [3, 7].
